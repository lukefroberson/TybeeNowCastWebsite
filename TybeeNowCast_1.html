About This System

    

      

        
How Nowcasting Works

        
TybeeNowCast uses statistical predictive models (logistic regression and random forest) to estimate the probability of an Enterococcus exceedance at each monitoring station before lab results are available — bypassing the typical 24–48 hour lag.

        
Environmental drivers including rainfall, water temperature, salinity, turbidity, tide stage, and wind direction are used as predictors, following the EPA's Six Key Steps framework for predictive beach water quality modeling.

      

      

        
Monitoring Stations

        
Five sampling stations are maintained by the Georgia Coastal Resources Division (CRD) along the Tybee Island shoreline, capturing distinct hydrological regimes.

        
North (TYN) · Middle (TYM) · South (TYS) face the open Atlantic. Polk Street (TYP) · Strand Street (TYST) are tidal creek stations with higher natural variability and historically elevated exceedance rates.

      

      

        
Indicator Organism

        
Enterococcus (CFU/100 mL) is the primary fecal indicator bacterium (FIB) used for marine recreational water quality. It is strongly associated with human pathogens and waterborne illness risk.

        
Georgia's advisory threshold of 70 CFU/100 mL aligns with EPA 2012 recreational water quality criteria. Values above this threshold indicate elevated health risk for swimmers and waders.

      

      

        
Data Sources

        
Samples are collected by Georgia CRD on approximately weekly intervals during the recreational season. Environmental covariates come from NOAA Station 8670870 and regional weather networks.

        
Rainfall uses a log₁₀ transformation with a 0.01-inch floor for zero-rain days. Model development follows EPA Six Key Steps for predictive beach water quality modeling (Francy et al., 2013).

Understanding

Advisory Levels Explained

    

      

        
🟢

        
Safe

        
≤ 70 CFU/100 mL

        
Enterococcus levels are within Georgia's recreational water quality standard. Swimming and wading carry normal, low background risk. Enjoy the beach!

      

      

        
🟡

        
Elevated — Use Caution

        
51–70 CFU/100 mL

        
Approaching the advisory threshold. Sensitive individuals — young children, the elderly, pregnant women, or anyone immunocompromised — should consider limiting full-contact water activities.

      

      

        
🔴

        
Advisory Issued

        
> 70 CFU/100 mL

        
The Georgia CRD advisory threshold has been exceeded. Full-body contact with the water (swimming, surfing) is not recommended. Avoid swallowing water. Rinse thoroughly after any contact.

      

    


    
    
The Science Behind the Numbers

    

      

        

          
🦠

          
What is Enterococcus?

        

        

          
Enterococcus is a group of bacteria naturally found in the digestive tracts of humans and warm-blooded animals. Its presence in coastal water signals contamination from fecal sources — including stormwater runoff, wildlife, and pet waste.

          
Because testing for every possible pathogen (viruses, parasites, other bacteria) in water is impractical, scientists use Enterococcus as a reliable indicator organism. High Enterococcus counts signal a higher likelihood that harmful pathogens may also be present.

          
Enterococcus is measured in Colony Forming Units per 100 milliliters (CFU/100 mL) of water — essentially counting bacterial colonies grown from a standard water sample in a lab.

        

      

      

        

          
🌧️

          
Why Does Rain Matter So Much?

        

        

          
Rainfall is one of the strongest predictors of elevated Enterococcus. Here's why: when it rains, stormwater washes across roads, lawns, and marshes, picking up bacteria from animal waste, failing septic systems, and overflowing storm drains — all of which can flow into coastal waters.

          
The impact isn't immediate. Antecedent rainfall (rain from the previous 1–3 days) matters as much as same-day rain — contamination pulses can persist in the water column for 24–72 hours after a rain event.

          
This is why TybeeNowCast uses both day-of rainfall and 3-day cumulative rainfall as model inputs, and why post-storm swimming carries elevated risk even on sunny days.

        

      

      

        

          
🌊

          
How Tides & Location Affect Risk

        

        

          
Not all Tybee Island stations are alike. North, Middle, and South Beach face the open Atlantic and benefit from ocean flushing and dilution — historically maintaining the lowest exceedance rates (1.5–2.2%).

          
Polk Street (TYP) and Strand Street (TYST) are tidal creek stations, where water exchange is slower, bacteria can concentrate, and inputs from marsh drainage are more direct. These stations show 4.6–7.5% exceedance rates — three to four times higher than the oceanfront beaches.

          
Tide stage also matters: flooding tides can push creek water and associated bacteria onto beach areas, while ebbing tides may carry freshwater runoff out from the marsh.

        

      

      

        

          
🌡️

          
Temperature, Salinity & Turbidity

        

        

          
Water temperature affects bacterial survival: warmer waters can support longer bacterial persistence and growth, while cooler winter water tends to limit Enterococcus concentrations. Georgia's subtropical climate means monitoring is important year-round.

          
Salinity acts as a natural barrier — Enterococcus struggles to persist in high-salinity ocean water. Reduced salinity following heavy rainfall (freshwater dilution) can allow bacteria to survive longer.

          
Turbidity (water cloudiness) often correlates with bacterial levels — both tend to rise after storms. Visibly murky water after rainfall is a practical visual cue to exercise extra caution.

        

      

    


    
    
Who Is Most Vulnerable?
Higher risk does not mean no swimming — it means informed decisions

    

      

        
👶

        
Young Children

        
Children under 5 are more likely to swallow water while playing and have developing immune systems. Keep toddlers from putting hands in mouths after water contact.

      

      

        
🧓

        
Older Adults

        
Adults 65+ may have reduced immune response to waterborne pathogens. Consider avoiding full-contact water activities when advisories are in effect.

      

      

        
🤰

        
Pregnant Women

        
Waterborne infections can pose greater health risks during pregnancy. Wading in calm, low-risk conditions is generally fine; avoid swimming during active advisories.

      

      

        
💊

        
Immunocompromised

        
People undergoing chemotherapy, organ transplant recipients, or those with HIV/AIDS have reduced ability to fight bacterial infections from water exposure.

      

      

        
🩹

        
Open Wounds

        
Cuts, abrasions, or surgical wounds provide a direct pathway for bacteria. Avoid water contact with open wounds during or after rain events.

      

      

        
🐾

        
Pets

        
Dogs are also susceptible to waterborne illness. Pets that drink saltwater or swim at tidal creek stations during elevated conditions can get sick. Rinse pets after any beach visit.

      

    


    
    
Georgia's Monitoring Program

    

      
How Water Quality Is Monitored at Tybee Island

      

        
Lead Agency
Georgia Coastal Resources Division (CRD), part of the Dept. of Natural Resources

        
Sampling Frequency
Approximately weekly during the recreational season (spring through fall), biweekly in winter

        
Sample Processing
Water samples are cultured in a laboratory. Results are typically available within 24–48 hours of collection

        
Advisory Threshold
70 CFU/100 mL Enterococcus — aligned with EPA 2012 Recreational Water Quality Criteria

        
Advisory Process
When a sample exceeds the threshold, CRD issues a public advisory. Advisories are lifted after a follow-up sample shows acceptable levels

        
Where TybeeNowCast Fits
This system provides predictive estimates between sample collection events — a nowcast, not a replacement for official monitoring

      

    


    
    
How to Reduce Your Exposure
Simple steps make a real difference

    

      
01
Wait 48–72 hours after heavy rain
Bacteria from stormwater runoff can persist for days after rainfall ends. If it rained more than 0.5 inches in the last three days, elevated levels are more likely — especially at tidal creek stations.

      
02
Choose oceanfront beaches when possible
North, Middle, and South Beach have significantly lower historical exceedance rates than Polk Street and Strand Street. When conditions are uncertain, the Atlantic-facing beaches offer lower baseline risk.

      
03
Don't swallow the water
Most waterborne illness from beach swimming occurs through accidental ingestion. Teach children to keep their mouths closed while playing and to never drink ocean or creek water.

      
04
Rinse off after swimming
A fresh water rinse — especially around the eyes, ears, nose, and mouth — reduces the amount of bacteria on your skin. Most beachside showers are suitable for this purpose.

      
05
Wash hands before eating
Sand and water can both carry bacteria. Always wash hands thoroughly with soap before handling food at the beach — a simple step that prevents the majority of beach-related GI illness.

      
06
Check conditions before you go
Use TybeeNowCast's Forecast Tool to see estimated exceedance probabilities for current conditions, and always check the official Georgia CRD advisory page for posted warnings before visiting.

      
07
Keep pets' waste off the beach
Pet waste is a significant source of Enterococcus at coastal beaches. Always bag and properly dispose of pet waste — it directly reduces bacterial loading to coastal waters that affect everyone.

      
08
Turbid water is a warning sign
Murky, brown, or cloudy water after rainfall often indicates elevated bacteria along with the suspended sediment. Use visual cues as a practical, real-time guide — if the water looks brown, don't swim.
